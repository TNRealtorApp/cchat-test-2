This year's tiniest contenders

Possumtail

Briar County

Population: 47

Officially recognized as a town only by the family that lives there. Town meetings happen on the porch unless it's raining, in which case they happen on a different porch.

Editor's pick

Cricket Hollow

Pawpaw County

Population: 312

Named for the noise, not the sport. The single traffic light has been yellow since 1994, which the residents consider both a feature and a metaphor.

Lukewarm Springs

Switchback County

Population: 88

A wellness destination for people with extremely modest expectations. The springs are tepid year-round; the brochures call this "consistent."

Most disappointing spa

Buckwheat Crossing

Holler County

Population: 619

The largest town in this guide, technically a metropolis by Tiny Tennessee standards. Boasts a hardware store, a different hardware store, and a feud between them dating to a 1981 lawnmower transaction.

Hogjaw Bend

Crawdad County

Population: 23

More of a curve in the road than a town, but the curve has its own zip code and a strong opinion about the curve two miles down. The welcome sign is hand-lettered and updated whenever someone moves out.

Frequently asked, occasionally answered

Is Tiny Tennessee a real travel service?

No. We are imaginary. So are the towns. So, in some sense, are the cricket population estimates.

How do you choose which towns to feature?

An exacting editorial process: a town qualifies if its population would fit on one school bus and its post office shares a building with at least one other business. Bonus points if that other business is a tackle shop.

Can I visit?

You can certainly try. Bring a paper map, a full tank, and the calm acceptance that GPS will give up roughly four miles before you arrive.

What if I'm from a real Tennessee town?

We salute you. None of the towns on this page are yours; any resemblance is the kind of coincidence that happens when you make up names involving possums.

Tiny Tennessee is satirical fiction. Towns named here are imaginary; any resemblance to real Tennessee places is coincidental.
